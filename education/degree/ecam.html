Individual Project

Modelling and Experimental Validation of a Multi-Electrode Electrochemical Additive Manufacturing System using Sequential Pulsed Deposition

Grade: 84.5%

Electrochemical Additive Manufacturing (ECAM) provides a low-energy, room-temperature pathway for metal 3D printing, but suffers from slow deposition rates due to ion depletion around a single electrode. My project explored whether a multi-electrode ECAM configuration, fired sequentially, could improve deposition uniformity and quality while reducing these ion-transport limitations.

Objectives

  • Simulate multi-electrode behaviour to predict current density and ion depletion zones.
  • Design and prototype a 2×1 ECAM printer capable of sequential pulsed deposition.
  • Experimentally assess how pulse duration and electrode spacing affect copper deposition morphology.
  • Evaluate whether sequential firing can mitigate diffusion-limited aggregation (DLA) and improve surface quality.

Approach

  • Built a custom 3D MATLAB simulation to examine electric-field interactions between electrodes and identify minimum spacing to avoid stray deposition.
  • Designed and manufactured a low-cost ECAM test rig, including a modular electrode array, 3D-printed fixtures, and Arduino-based pulse control.
  • Performed controlled copper deposition experiments varying inter-electrode gap, firing sequence, and pulse duration (0.05–4 s).
  • Analysed all deposits using 3D focus-variation microscopy and custom MATLAB image/point-cloud processing.

Key Findings

  • Simulation showed that at least 1 mm spacing is required for independent electric fields and clean operation.
  • 0.5 s pulsed sequential firing improved surface roughness by ~16% vs. single-electrode control, producing more compact, uniform deposits.
  • Long pulses (≥2 s) caused dendritic growth due to ion depletion—confirming the benefit of short recovery periods.
  • Very short pulses (0.05–0.25 s) produced dense, smooth nodules and more accurate deposit diameters.
  • Major experimental challenge: immersion deposition on brass electrodes caused debris, inconsistent geometries, and degraded repeatability.

Outcome

The project successfully delivered the first verified proof-of-concept for a sequentially pulsed, multi-electrode ECAM system using simple hardware. Results demonstrate that sequential firing improves deposition quality and can counteract diffusion-limited effects—offering a promising path toward scalable, low-cost electrochemical 3D printing. Future work includes automated Z-axis control, inert microelectrodes (e.g., platinum), improved electrolyte flow, and scaling to larger electrode arrays.
