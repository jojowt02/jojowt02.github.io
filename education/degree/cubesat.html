Degree Project

CubeSat Mission Design – HyperScout-2 Volcano Monitoring

As part of the Spacecraft Systems & Design (MMME4038) module, I designed a CubeSat mission to monitor volcanic activity in Japan using the HyperScout-2 hyperspectral imager. The project covered payload-driven spacecraft architecture, subsystem integration, orbit selection, power and thermal modelling, and a complete mission-level CONOPS.

Mission Overview

Mission Objectives

The mission’s purpose was to detect volcanic temperature anomalies, gas signatures, and surface morphology changes using hyperspectral imaging. Core engineering goals included:

  • Integrating HyperScout-2 within 6U constraints (mass, power, thermal, volume).
  • Designing an operations schedule for daily imaging passes over Japan.
  • Selecting an orbit balancing revisit, lighting conditions, and power availability.
  • Ensuring sufficient downlink capacity for high-volume hyperspectral datasets.

Payload

HyperScout-2 Imager

HyperScout-2 is a compact hyperspectral + thermal imaging instrument, capable of capturing detailed spectral signatures essential for volcanic monitoring. The payload strongly influenced the spacecraft design, driving pointing requirements, thermal management, and data handling.

  • Hyperspectral, multispectral and thermal imaging combined.
  • Low electrical power, but high data generation → communication bottleneck.
  • Thermal stability essential for accurate spectral response.
  • Wide field of view → optimised for regional volcanic mapping.

Concept of Operations

Mission CONOPS

The mission lifecycle was built around predictable imaging opportunities and efficient use of power and communication windows.

  • Sun-synchronous low-Earth orbit ensuring consistent lighting and repeat coverage.
  • Autonomous payload warm-up, calibration, and imaging when over Japan.
  • Onboard storage of hyperspectral cubes → S-band downlink during ground passes.
  • Fallback safe-mode for attitude and thermal protection.

Spacecraft Architecture

Subsystem Design Summary

Power System (EPS)
Deployable solar panels + regulated battery system, sized around payload peak demand and S-band transmission windows.

ADCS
Three-axis control using reaction wheels and magnetorquers, ensuring stable nadir pointing for hyperspectral imaging.

Communications
UHF/VHF command link paired with high-rate S-band downlink to accommodate large hyperspectral data volumes.

Thermal Control
Passive elements (MLI, coatings) combined with payload and battery heaters to maintain operational temperature ranges.

Structure & Layout
Internal design prioritised payload FOV, thermal spacing, centre-of-gravity alignment, and ease of I&T procedures.

Engineering Considerations

Key Challenges

  • Downlink bandwidth vs hyperspectral cube size.
  • Thermal management during imaging and eclipse periods.
  • Maintaining power margins with limited panel area.
  • Payload-driven attitude stability requirements.
  • Ensuring robust autonomy with limited ground contact time.

Reflection

Outcome & Learning

This project strengthened my ability to design spacecraft systems under real constraints, perform requirement flowdown, and justify design trade-offs. Working with HyperScout-2 provided practical insight into payload-driven engineering and the interplay between subsystem capabilities, mission objectives, and operational feasibility.
