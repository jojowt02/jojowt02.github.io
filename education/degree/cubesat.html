Degree Project

CubeSat Mission Design – HyperScout-2 Volcano Monitoring

As part of the Spacecraft Systems & Design (MMME4038) module, I developed a full CubeSat mission concept designed to monitor volcanic activity in Japan. The mission centres on the HyperScout-2 hyperspectral imager, requiring a complete end-to-end engineering breakdown: payload integration, subsystem architecture, power and thermal considerations, communication strategy, operational planning, and overall system justification.


Mission Objectives

The goal of the mission was to reliably detect thermal anomalies, gas signatures, and surface changes around active Japanese volcanoes using hyperspectral imaging. Core objectives included:

  • Integrating HyperScout-2 into a 6U CubeSat bus within mass, power, and thermal constraints.
  • Designing a feasible CONOPS supporting daily observation passes.
  • Selecting an orbit that maximises revisit frequency over volcanic regions.
  • Ensuring downlink capability for high-volume hyperspectral datasets.

Payload: HyperScout-2

HyperScout-2 is a compact hyperspectral and thermal imaging instrument capable of capturing detailed spectral signatures critical for volcanic monitoring. Its wide field of view and spectral depth enable detection of pre-eruption indicators such as gas release and thermal hotspots.

  • Hyperspectral + multispectral capabilities in a CubeSat-compatible form.
  • Low-power imaging but high data generation, influencing communications design.
  • Requires strict thermal management and stable nadir pointing.

Concept of Operations (CONOPS)

The mission lifecycle was structured around predictable imaging opportunities and efficient downlink scheduling. Key elements included:

  • Operating in a sun-synchronous low-Earth orbit for consistent lighting conditions.
  • Payload warm-up, calibration, and imaging cycles triggered during passes over Japan.
  • Onboard storage of hyperspectral data followed by S-band downlink during ground-station windows.
  • Autonomous safe-mode transitions to maintain spacecraft health.

Subsystem Design Summary

Power System (EPS)
Deployable solar panels and a regulated battery architecture were sized around peak payload demands, including thermal control and S-band transmission.

ADCS
A three-axis stabilised system with reaction wheels and magnetorquers met the pointing requirements for stable Hyperscout-2 imaging.

Communications
High data throughput required an S-band downlink paired with a UHF/VHF command uplink.

Thermal Control
Mixed active–passive strategies (MLI, heaters, payload temperature conditioning) ensured thermal stability.

Structural & Mechanical
Internal layout prioritised payload field-of-view, thermal spacing, and centre-of-gravity alignment.

Key Engineering Challenges

  • Balancing hyperspectral data volume with available downlink capacity.
  • Managing thermal loads on the payload across varying orbital conditions.
  • Choosing an orbit optimised for revisit while retaining CubeSat feasibility.
  • Maintaining adequate power margins during peak operations.
  • Ensuring robust autonomous behaviour with limited ground contact.

Outcome & Reflection

This project strengthened my understanding of spacecraft systems engineering, requirement flowdown, and subsystem integration. Working with a real commercial payload, even in a theoretical context, helped develop practical engineering judgement—balancing feasibility, performance, and mission constraints. It also deepened my appreciation for payload-driven spacecraft architecture and end-to-end mission reasoning.
