Future Aerospace Technology Mini-Project

Sustainable Aviation Fuel and the Future of Air Travel

For the MMME3075 Management and Professional Practice module, I completed an individual research project titled “An Overview of SAF and its Implementation in the Current and Future Aviation Industry”. The report critically examined Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) as a near- and long-term route to decarbonising aviation, exploring fuel chemistry, certification pathways, production scaling and policy, as well as the limits of competing technologies.

Module Context

Management & Professional Practice (MMME3075)

This coursework formed a major part of the MMME3075 assessment, requiring an unsupervised investigation into a new or rapidly advancing aerospace technology. Students were asked to research one of several technology areas and produce a 2,500–3,000 word professional report that:

  • Justified the importance of the chosen technology in a real aerospace context.
  • Reviewed the underlying technology, development history and current challenges.
  • Assessed prospects for further development, market adoption and competing options.
  • Presented clear, reasoned conclusions and recommendations.

I chose to focus on the role of SAF in making future air travel more sustainable, aligning with my interest in propulsion, systems engineering and the environmental challenge facing commercial aviation.

Research Focus

Scope of the Investigation

The report provided a structured overview of SAF from first principles through to long-term deployment, broken down into:

  • Definition and composition: Clarifying how Sustainable Aviation Fuels differ from conventional jet fuels (CJF), including the role of n-alkanes, iso-alkanes, cycloalkanes and aromatics in present Jet A-1 fuel.
  • Certification and “drop-in” compatibility: Explaining the requirements of ASTM D1655, D7566 and D4054, and how they govern which synthetic fuels can be blended, reclassified and used within the existing aviation fuel system infrastructure and current aircraft.
  • SAF production pathways: Reviewing current approved conversion pathways (e.g. HEFA, FT-SPK, ATJ-SPK, HC-HEFA) and the feedstocks they rely on, including waste oils, agricultural by-products and algae.
  • Scaling production: Analysing present SAF production volumes, competition with renewable diesel, and the feedstock availability needed to reach 2050 targets.
  • Policy and market drivers: Assessing how legislation and incentives such as ReFuelEU and the Sustainable Skies Act influence refinery choices, airline offtake agreements and ticket pricing.

Technical Insights

Key Findings from the Report

A central part of the work was examining what SAF can and cannot improve when constrained to be a drop-in fuel compatible with the existing fleet and infrastructure. Some of the key insights included:

  • Aromatics and emissions: Conventional Jet A-1 typically contains around 21 wt.% aromatics to meet density and seal-swelling requirements. Reducing this fraction lowers soot and particulate emissions but is limited by compatibility with existing seals and systems.
  • Drop-in boundary: ASTM D7566 currently sets a minimum aromatic content of 8% for drop-in blends, meaning SAF can improve but not eliminate aromatic-related emissions in the near term.
  • Certification cost and timescales: Full approval of a new pathway under D4054 requires large volumes of fuel for multi-tier testing and can cost several million dollars over a period of years, creating a barrier for new producers.
  • Production imbalance: Despite growing interest, SAF currently represents a tiny fraction of global jet fuel use, and refineries often favour renewable diesel due to existing incentives and infrastructure.
  • Algae as a future feedstock: The report identified algae-derived oils (e.g. Botryococcus braunii) as a promising long-term feedstock for SAF, with potential to reach very high global production if technical and economic hurdles are overcome.

Policy & Outlook

Short-Term Challenges and Long-Term Ambitions

The report argued that while SAF is currently more expensive than conventional jet fuel, regulatory pressure and policy incentives are shifting the economic balance:

  • Mandates: European measures such as ReFuelEU introduce progressively increasing minimum SAF blend requirements, driving demand even when costs are high.
  • Incentives: Instruments like the Sustainable Skies Act in the U.S. offer tax credits per gallon of SAF, linked to lifecycle emissions reductions, improving the business case for producers.
  • Blending limits: Current standards typically restrict SAF blends to 50% with conventional jet fuel, but the report notes work underway to enable 100% SAF use as technology and experience mature.

The overall conclusion was that, while still in its infancy, SAF is the only technologically mature, system-compatible option capable of delivering large near-term reductions in aviation lifecycle emissions, provided that production can be scaled through new feedstocks, investment and policy support.

Result

Assessment Outcome & Feedback

The coursework was awarded a mark of 85.5%, reflecting top-tier performance across research depth, critical analysis and quality of presentation.

  • Sound research: Marker praised the report as being underpinned by an “impressive amount of research”, with authoritative sources including journal articles, international standards and policy papers.
  • Critical analysis: The discussion was described as “very well reasoned throughout”, with clear links between legislation, technology maturity, consumer behaviour and SAF adoption.
  • Writing & formatting: Feedback highlighted that the report was “outstandingly professionally written… comparable to a decent journal article”, with clear structure and high-quality figures and layout.

This piece of work solidified my interest in the intersection between aerospace technology, sustainability, policy and real-world implementation.
